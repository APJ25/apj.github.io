Operating Modes & Theory

The disadvantage of the conventional PSFB converter is that it has significant conduction losses during the freewheeling interval. If the turns ratio is fixed, as the input voltage increases, the duty cycle has to be reduced to get the required output voltage, which means more duration for freewheeling current and more conduction losses. To overcome this issue, a multi-transformer system is used.

Series Mode

When the input voltage increases, switching to the series mode of operation doubles the turns ratio, thereby maintaining the duty cycle. The series mode of operation is exactly the same as the traditional phase shift converter where the middle branch of the reconfigurable PSFB is completely switched off and the phase shift is between the available two legs of switches. The output voltage is given by:

Vout = 2 · Deff · (Ns/Np) · (Vin/NT)

Parallel Mode

Parallel mode of operation occurs when middle-leg transistors are employed in the switching scheme. Mosfets Q1, Q4, and Q5 are switched together to provide the parallel mode of operation. Switching Q2, Q3, and Q6 will cause the current to flow to the converter in the opposite direction. The middle leg will be out of phase, but the first and third legs’ devices are in phase. The output voltage is given by:

Vout = 2 · Deff · (Ns/Np) · (Vin/NTP)
