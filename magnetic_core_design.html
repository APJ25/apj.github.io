Design of Transformer

The aim of the design is to optimize the performance of the transformer with lower losses and lower cost. This is achieved by tuning the number of turns within permissible limits to observe a trend in loss calculation. The process ensures that the flux density and maximum allowable loss due to thermal limitations remain within their permissible limits.

This iterative process, performed across the entire core dataset using MATLAB, allows for the identification of the most preferable core and the corresponding optimal number of turns for that core.

Series Case Operation

In the series mode of operation, the primary windings are connected in series, while the secondary sides are connected in parallel. The primary windings are twisted in opposite directions (one clockwise, the other anti-clockwise). The maximum input voltage that can be applied is 160 V.

The voltage gain of the series system of a phase-shift full-bridge converter is given as:

Vout = 2 · Deff · K · Vin

where K is the turns ratio of the transformer. The flux distribution is shown in the figures below.

Using the right-hand thumb rule, the flux in the left leg is in the upward direction, and the flux in the right leg is in the downward direction. In the central leg, the fluxes cancel out. From the figure, the effective path length of the E core is:

lm = 2a + 2b

The loop equation is given as:

Np1I1 + Ns1I2 + Np2I1 + Ns2I2 − ϕR = 0

This simplifies to:

2Np1I1 + 2Ns1I2 − ϕR = 0

The magnetic flux, flux density, magnetic field strength, and inductances can be derived from the following equations:

ϕ = (2Np1I1 + 2Ns1I2) / R
B = (Vp · DTs) / ((2Np1 + 2Ns1) Ac)
H = (2Np1I1 + 2Ns1I2) / lm
L11 = 4N2p1I1 / R

Parallel Mode Operation

In the parallel mode of operation, both the primary and secondary windings are connected in parallel. The primary windings are twisted in the same direction. The maximum input voltage that can be applied is 80V.

Using the right-hand thumb rule, the flux in both the left and right legs is in the upward direction. In the central leg, the fluxes add together. The effective path length of the E core is:

lm = 3a + 4b

The various parameters can be derived from the following equations:

ϕ = (Ns1I2) / [ RCR / (RC + 2R) ]
B = (Vp · DTs) / ((2Ns1)Ac)
L11 = 4N2p1I1 / R

Inductor

Design Theory and Optimization

The traditional methods of area product and core geometry coefficient method involve an iterative, trial-and-error process of choosing a suitable core, estimating the turns, and calculating the air gap for the required inductance. These methods often overlook the interdependence of magnetic flux density, number of turns, inductance, and air gap. The design method proposed here does not require selecting a core in the first place. Instead, this method starts with solving Ampere’s law that relates the dependency between inductance, number of turns, and air gap. This approach allows for a better convergence time and provides high design flexibility.

Calculation of Inductance

The inductance is calculated based on the equation below. The ripple current is taken to be 10% of the output current (6.6 A). Since only 80 V is expressed across each winding, the input voltage (Vin) is taken as 80 V and the turns ratio (K) is 3. The diode drop (VD) is taken to be 0.85 V considering Schottky diodes. The inductance is found to be 12.7 μH.

Lf = Vo / (2*fs * delta_Io) * (1 - Vo / (Vin * K - VD))

Optimization of Inductor

The solution of the two equations, namely the air gap equation and the reluctance model of inductance, gives the number of turns and the required air gap length.

lg = (mu_0 * N * Idc / Bdc) - (lc / mu_r)
L = (mu_0 * mu_r * N^2 * Ac) / (lc + mu_r * lg)

In the air gap equation, the term lc/mu_r is very small since the relative permeability of ferrite materials is around 2000, so it is taken to be zero in the design. The conductor cross-section area is the same as the windings used in the secondary of the transformer. The Bmax of the core is limited to 0.2 T and Imax is taken to be 6.6 A plus the ripple, which is 7.26 A. The core diameter is the variable, varied from 3 mm to 14 mm. The solution of the equations gives the number of turns and air gap as:

(N, lg) = ( (L * delta_Imax) / (Ac * delta_Bmax), (mu_0 * N^2 * L) / (Ac * Bmax^2) - (lc / mu_r) )

As the number of turns increases, the choice of air gap increases linearly with it. The choice of cross-sectional area with air gap is inversely proportional. The advantage this method gives is that all the possibilities of area are available, not like the other methods where the area of core is fixed for a particular core.

Now the number of turns identified above is utilized to calculate the winding area (Aw) required for the winding. The Ku is chosen to be 0.78. The mean core length (path length) is calculated using the subsequent equation.

Aw = (N * area_of_conductor) / Ku
Lc = pi * (sqrt( (4*Aw) / (0.78*pi) ) + Dc/2)

The optimal solution is identified by calculating the total losses as explained in the case of transformer design, where total loss is the sum of core losses and copper losses. The core losses are calculated from the Steinmetz’s empirical equation and the DC losses are only considered here for copper losses since the effect of AC resistance is very small. The change in flux density is taken to be 20% of Bmax. The optimum number of turns is the position where the Pareto front of total loss to core volume is closest to the vertical and horizontal intersections of the extreme points. The number of turns, area of the core and the window area corresponding to this optimum point leads to the optimized inductor design.

Inductor

The EE19/8/9 cores made of 3C94 material are utilized. The inductor is made up of three parallel-connected AWG 25 wires with ten turns coiled on the center limb. The air gap is created by sandwiching insulation between the two EE sections, and the overall air gap is checked. R31 polyester tape is used as insulation. The insulating tape is used to bind the two core components together. Prior to testing, the wires’ continuity was examined.

Inductor Modelling and Simulation

The toroidal form is used to match the design in the Ansys 2D model of the inductor and furthermore it makes the modelling easier. The air gap, core cross-section diameter, and number of turns are parametrized and corresponding inductance is measured. The core cross-section diameter is varied from 6 mm to 12 mm, the air gap is varied between 0.5 mm to 1 mm, and the number of turns is varied between 8 to 14. The conductors are modelled as yellow circular rings. The outer ring is given the current excitation of 7.2 A with a positive current direction and the inner ring with a negative current direction. The depth setup was also parametrized by varying it as the same as the core cross-section diameter.

The test gave the output such that the combination of 7 mm core diameter, air gap of 0.5 mm and 10 number of turns gave an inductance closer to 12 μH.
