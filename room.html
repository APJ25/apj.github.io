Clean Room Report: P-N Junction

Mid Sweden University - ET007A Sensors and Instrumentation

Authors: Emmanuel Ndagijimana, Davide Viggiani, Taian Hu, Bingsong Wu, Adarsh Pulimoottil Jolly

Lecturer: Börje Norlin | Submission Date: 12/12/2023

Introduction

Processing a PN diode in a cleanroom involves a series of steps to fabricate the diode with precision and reliability. Cleanrooms provide an environment where there are very few particles in the air. This room is equipped with yellow lighting, to minimize the exposure of photoresist materials to certain wavelengths of light, particularly ultraviolet (UV) light. The specialty of the lithographical technique used in the PN junction processing is photolithography. Photolithography is a fundamental process in semiconductor fabrication, and its specialty lies in its ability to precisely define patterns on a wafer surface with high resolution. Resolution in photolithography refers to the ability of the technique to reproduce fine details and patterns accurately on the wafer surface. To determine the resolution in the below described PN junction processing, specific information about the wavelength of the UV light source, the type of photomask, and the characteristics of the photoresist would be necessary. The quality of the lithography patterns after exposure was evaluated using microscopy. Below is a detailed explanation of the photolithography process specifically done while PN junction was being processed.

Processing of PN junction

Development of P-type Region

Wafer preparation

Inspected the available wafers for any defects or contamination. Ensured that the wafers met the required specifications for size, thickness, and orientation. The oxide thickness was controlled through processes like thermal oxidation. During thermal oxidation, the wafer was exposed to elevated temperatures in the presence of oxygen, leading to the growth of a silicon dioxide layer.

Photoresist application

HMDS (Hexamethyldisilazane) was typically applied before the deposition of photoresist to improve adhesion. A positive photoresist (type 1818) was spin-coated onto the frontside of a silicon wafer at a speed of 2000 RPM, aiming to achieve a final thickness of 1.8 μm.

Baking

The wafer was baked at 115°C for 1 minute to remove residual solvents and promote adhesion. The photoresist was then applied to the backside of the wafer, followed by a furnace-based thermal treatment for 15 minutes to stabilize the resist.

Alignment and Masking

The wafer was placed on a photolithography aligner. A photomask containing the pattern for boron doping was aligned with the wafer and exposed to UV light. A positive photoresist was used, making the exposed areas soluble.

Development and Rinsing

The wafer was immersed in a developer solution for 1 minute to selectively dissolve the exposed regions of the photoresist. Following development, the wafer was rinsed for two minutes with deionized water to remove any remaining developer and residue.

Inspection, Etching, and Stripping

The pattern quality was inspected under a microscope. The wafer was then etched with a buffered HF solution to remove silicon dioxide in the exposed areas, creating hydrophobic openings. Finally, the remaining photoresist was stripped away, and the wafer was cleaned.

Annealing

The wafer was loaded into a doping furnace with boron at 950°C. This high-temperature process allowed the P-type dopant atoms to diffuse into the silicon lattice, forming the P-type region of the PN junction.

Metallization

The metallization process involves creating electrical contacts on the semiconductor device. This included opening P-areas with an HF solution, evaporating a 3000 Å layer of aluminum, and using a second photolithography step (Patterning Mask 2) to define the metal contacts. After etching the unwanted aluminum, another aluminum layer was evaporated on the backside for grounding. The process concluded with stripping the resist and a final forming gas anneal to optimize the electrical properties.

Characterization

I-V Characteristics

The I-V characteristics of 1mm, 2mm, and 4mm side squares of the pn-junction were measured using a 4-wire method to eliminate errors from conductor resistance. The breakdown voltage for the diode was determined to be -84V.

C-V Characteristics

The depletion voltage, where the diode begins to conduct, was measured for each size: 0.32V (1mm), 0.29V (2mm), and 0.26V (4mm). From the C-V curves, the junction capacitance was determined, which allowed for the calculation of the depletion depth.
