Transformer and Inductor Testing Results

Transformer Testing Results

Open Circuit Test

The open circuit test was performed by applying a variable pulse voltage to the primary windings while the secondary windings were left open. This allowed for the characterization of the transformer's no-load performance, including core losses (visualized in the B-H curves) and magnetization characteristics. The following instruments were utilized during the test: a programmable DC power supply, a high-frequency oscilloscope to capture waveforms, a current probe for accurate current measurement, and thermocouples connected to a data logger to monitor the temperature rise on the transformer core legs.

Short Circuit Test

The short circuit test for each mode of operation on 42 and 55 EE cores gave a maximum current of roughly 5 A for 15 V in the series cases and 10 A for 15 V in the parallel case. Inductor Results

The frequency analysis of the two inductors gave the inductance and capacitance for the range of frequencies from 100 Hz to 1MHz. At the 100KHz operating range, both the inductors were showing inductance close to 12 µH, and the corresponding capacitance be closer to 0 F. The phase plot showed a phase value closer to 90° at 100KHz and the magnitude plot showed a magnitude around 18dB.

Gate Pulse Verification

The gate pulse for the system was provided using the launchpad F28379D and the gate pulses across the mosfets were tested. The oscilloscope showed the top channel gate pulse to be lagging the second branch and the bottom channel gate pulse to be leading the first branch of mosfet switches. The phase shift is present in the gate pulses and using the monitor option in Simulink the PWM blocks with phase shift was validated at real time.

Discussion

The B-H loop curves show consistent behaviour for the cores, with losses increasing with an increase in applied voltage. The figure generated from the B-H curves by calculating the loop area that corresponds to the losses shows that EE42 has lower losses compared to the EE50 core at all tested voltages in both modes of operation.

The temperature of the system during open-loop testing is higher for the EE42 core in both modes of operation than the EE55 core. The temperature differences between the three legs in the series scenario of operation is not significant at first. However, as the voltages grow, the middle leg becomes less hot relative to the outer legs. This demonstrates that the system operates in a series mode, with no flux in the middle leg. Thermal conduction from the outer legs causes the middle leg’s temperature to rise over time. In terms of temperature, the worst-case situation for an EE42 in series occurs at 272 V, where the temperature approaches 62°C.

The EE42 operating in parallel mode shows temperature rise through losses. Because of its greater volume, the middle leg has a larger thermal resistance, which results in a higher temperature there. This temperature change is clearly visible at higher voltages. At a higher operating voltage of 114 V, the parallel case temperature rises to 75°C. In terms of temperature rise in both parallel and series operation, the EE55 exhibits the same trend as the EE42. However, the temperature only rises by 70°C in parallel and 50°C in series.

Even though this is the case, the temperature is within the limits of the system for EE42 at the operating region. The short circuit test results show a similar case where the temperature of the windings rises but is within the safety margin of 75°C. The parallel case windings suffer more stress from temperature rise, which is as expected since the full load current is higher in this case of operation. The insulation was safe at this operation point, showing that the winding integrity is good.

The final choice of transformer core comes down to the fact that EE42 suffers lesser losses at the operating voltage. Even though the temperature is a little bit high, it is still within the safety margin. Other than the loss factor, EE42 having a smaller volume reduces the footprint requirement with a base area of 860 mm², whereas EE55 has a base area of 1180.2 mm². Furthermore, using a single EE42 instead of the two ETD39 core setup reduces footprint requirement. This shows considerable improvement in the power density of the system. Even though the values of losses are a little bit lower comparing the numerical approach, this is expected since the core loss is calculated using the generalized Steinmetz Equation.

The inductor designed has the required inductance around 12.7 µH. There is a small difference in the value due to the inefficiency in manually adjusting the air gap. The phase margin of 85° at 100 KHz is showing consistent performance, whereas at low frequency there is a reversal in the phases of the two inductors. Even though this is the case, the phase starts to have the relevant values from 10KHz.

The MOSFET gate point showed the correct gate pulse wave. The investigation into the ZVS approach could not be done with the practical testing of the reconfigurable PSFB converter due to time limitations. However, the transformer and inductor were designed to meet the ZVS criteria. As a result, the magnetic components remain valid and appropriate for future experimental validation.
